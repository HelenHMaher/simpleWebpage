The Blob

A many-headed slime that came before us and just might change the world.

Overview

First considered a fungus, the Physarum polycephalum (literally translated to "many-headed slime") was reclassified in the 1990s as a slime-mold. One of the easiest eukaryotic microbs to grow and maintain in a lab setting, it is often used to study the movement of single-celled organisms. Having developed some 500 million years before the human, The Blob is one of the most resiliant forms of life on the planet. Neigh impossible to kill, it can hibernate for long periods of time, even surviving a trip in through the microwave. However, when conditions are favorable, it will grow a break-neck speeds, sometimes doubling in size overnight.

Recent Popularity

The Blob has garnered increasing media attention in recent years when it began being used in increasingly complicated and visually impressive experiments. The Blob moves slowly, meer centimeters over the course of an hour, but it does move and despite having no brain or central nervous system, it shows signs of being able to not only learn, but to remember. When subjected to extreme conditions of temperature as regular intervals, The Blob began anticipating the onset of those conditions at those intervals. The Blob has been found capable of solving mazes, and even reproducing transportation systems. In a 2010 paper, oatflakes were used to represent Tokyo and the surrounding towns and The Blob created a network similar to the train system being used there today.

When The Blob was introduced to the Paris Zoo in October of 2019, it was to much excitement. But because this scientific curiousity and organic marvel is relatively unassuming in its speed and flamboyance, the exhibit will also include filmed footage of its problem-solving accomplishments.

Relevance to Bio-computing

Because of its ability to construct networks, scientists have been very interested in The Blob's potential in the area of bio-computing. It has been reported that The Blob has show the ability to logic gates. These reports have yet to be peer-reviewed and any model that would allow for actual computation remains mostly theoretical, but the groundwork is being layed. Much research remains to be done.
